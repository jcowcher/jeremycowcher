> "There is something very powerful about zero. Zero is a better number than one dollar ... Zero is a good number."
>
> — Jeff Bezos ([Squawk Pod](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NSVpd46EfLo))

> "Anyone can become an expert at anything in six months, whether it is hydrodynamics for boats or cyclonic systems for vacuum cleaners."
>
> — James Dyson ([Against the Odds: An Autobiography](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/955045.Against_the_Odds))

James Dyson didn't train as an engineer. He went to art school. He taught himself the hydrodynamics and the cyclonic physics he needed by reading, asking, and building, because the thing he wanted to make required it. His claim that anyone can become an expert in six months isn't motivational fluff; it's a description of how he actually worked. The barrier to entry in most fields is lower than the insiders want you to believe, and focused effort beats credentials more often than not.

I believe him because I've now lived a small version of it. A year ago I couldn't really code. Today I've built and shipped real things. The gap between those two states wasn't a degree or a bootcamp; it was deciding to start and then doing it badly until I did it less badly. The courses I took beforehand turned out to be mostly unnecessary. What mattered was building something I cared about and learning whatever that thing demanded along the way.

What's changed is the clock. Dyson's six months assumed you had to find the books, decode the jargon, and wait for someone qualified to answer your question. AI collapses most of that. The tool that once intimidated you can now explain itself to you, in your words, at your pace, exactly when you're stuck. When I hit something I don't understand, I don't lose a day hunting for the answer; I ask, I get it, and I keep moving. Six months of self-teaching compresses into something far shorter when the expert is sitting next to you the entire time.

This is the part of the AI story that gets least attention and may matter most. The headlines are about what AI can build for you. The quieter truth is what AI can teach you. It is the greatest tutor ever made available to a curious person: infinitely patient, available at midnight, and willing to explain the same thing five different ways until one of them lands. For anyone who ever put a subject in the "too hard" basket because the on-ramp looked too steep, that on-ramp just got a lot gentler.

There's a catch, and it's the whole reason for this series. Letting AI do the thing and letting AI teach you the thing are not the same, and it's easy to confuse them. If you only ever accept the output, you finish with a working result and no new ability, like watching someone else lift weights at the gym. The value compounds only when you treat each task as a chance to understand, not just to complete. The point isn't to avoid the work; it's to make sure the work leaves you more capable than it found you.

So that's where this series starts. Not with what I built, but with the conviction underneath it: that the ability to learn quickly is now the highest-leverage skill a person can have, and that AI has made it more available than at any point in history. Dyson proved you could do it in six months with library books and stubbornness. The interesting question now is what you can do with the same stubbornness and a tutor who never sleeps.
